00 — Executive Summary

Six judgments to anchor every decision that follows.

The product should not be sold as another employee monitor. Its real moat is that it sits inside the cloud desktop, sees how knowledge work actually happens across systems, and can turn that into workflow graphs, knowledge memory, SOPs, agent skills and compute-cost savings.

01 · Position
Rename the category, not just the feature.

Reposition from “Desktop Monitor / Employee Monitoring” to AI Work Intelligence Platform for Cloud Desktops. The category change unlocks executive, IT and AI budgets instead of HR-only spend.

02 · Market
Five adjacent markets, one center.

Time tracking, workforce analytics, insider-risk DLP, enterprise AI search, and process / task mining all overlap here. None of them owns the cloud-desktop layer; that is the wedge.

03 · Acceptance
Operations-heavy, system-rich teams first.

Highest acceptance comes from BPO, logistics back-office, insurance ops and finance shared services — workflows are repeatable, SOPs are valuable, and the buyer is the COO, not HR.

04 · Product
Seven modules, three product packs.

Capture · Understanding · Workflow · Knowledge · Personal Assistant · Manager Dashboard · Compute Optimization. Sold as Work Insight, Work Intelligence Pro, and Workflow / Knowledge / Agent Enterprise.

05 · Economics
Don’t price like Time Doctor.

Public benchmarks cluster at $9–$20/user/month for monitoring, $29–$35/user/month for AI assistants and insider-risk. Item should layer SaaS + private deployment + AI / storage usage + hardware service.

06 · Sequence
Ship the personal value first.

Start with AI daily summaries and project attribution that employees actually want to use. Workflow maps, SOP factory and knowledge connectors follow. Agent execution and compute governance are last.

I — Reframing the Product

From a recorder of screens to a graph of work.

Today’s system already does the hard part: a user works inside a VM, the system records the day, and AI summarises what that person did. That is well past traditional time tracking. The right next step is to convert that signal into structured work — projects, events, decisions, exceptions and skills — and to surround it with the email, drive and business systems that explain why the screen looks the way it does.

Screen activity → work events → workflow graph → SOP & knowledge → agent skill → automation. The core narrative

What the product actually is

LayerTraditional MonitorsThis Product
Data LayerTime, screenshots, URL / AppVM screen stream, window events, OCR / VLM, document & mail context, resource signals
AnalysisActivity score, productivity bucketsProject attribution, event chains, workflow paths, bottlenecks, anomalies, intent
KnowledgeEnterprise search or doc indexPersonal / team work memory and project knowledge graph with source citations
AutomationReports & remindersVideo-to-skill, agent skills, audited execution, automation recommendations
InfrastructureStand-alone VDI or recorderVM sizing, GPU sharing, AI inference scheduling, cold/hot storage tiering
Avoid · Bossware framing
The most expensive mistake is selling this as “we can watch your employees”. That framing caps the price, blocks HR/Legal and erodes employee trust before the product can demonstrate value. Frame it as understanding work, surfacing rework, generating SOPs, and giving every employee an AI work memory and daily summary they actually want.

Product naming

Among candidates, WorkGraph AI is recommended. It signals the graph nature of the data (work, knowledge, process), it scales beyond a single feature, and it survives translation to enterprise buyers. Acceptable alternates include Workflow Intelligence and Work Memory; Desktop Intelligence is precise but risks being read as a monitor.

II — Market & Competition

Five adjacent markets — and the gap between them.

The opportunity sits at the intersection of time tracking, workforce analytics, insider risk, enterprise AI search and process / task mining. Each is a multi-hundred-million to multi-billion dollar category. None of them owns the cloud-desktop signal end-to-end, which is what makes this product structurally different.

III — Enterprise Acceptance

Companies don’t refuse monitoring. They refuse opacity.

Field research and academic studies are consistent: enterprises will pay for visibility, but only when it is transparent, role-appropriate, and creates value the employee can also feel. The product must therefore lead with employee-visible benefits (work memory, daily summary, knowledge search) and offer manager and IT views as configurable overlays, not as the headline.

Speak the right language
Replace “we can watch employees” with “we help you understand how work actually gets done.” Replace “catch slackers” with “surface rework, bottlenecks and training gaps.” Replace “managers can replay an employee’s day” with “authorised reviewers can pull permissioned evidence for specific events.”

V — Workflow Intelligence

Don’t store screenshots. Store work events.

The long-term asset of this product is not a video archive. It is a structured graph of how work happens — sessions, projects, events, steps, decisions, exceptions and skills. Building the right schema from day one is what turns a recorder into a system of intelligence.

Event model

LayerDefinitionExample
SessionA continuous span of work09:15–10:02 handling ABC shipment exception
Project / CaseBusiness entityClient ABC · Order #12345 · Ticket #567
EventWhat happened in business termsAddress change · customer reply · invoice match
StepUI-level actionOpen WMS → search order → view inventory → update status
DecisionBranching judgementInventory insufficient → escalate to supervisor
ExceptionBlock, rework, errorSystem error · missing customer info · duplicate entry
SkillReusable capabilityAddress change · POD reissue · EDI failure triage

AI pipeline

screen video / keyframes
  └─ OCR + UI detection + active window + URL + app metadata
       └─ low-cost classifier: work / idle / meeting / system / sensitive
            └─ task segmentation: sessions and events
                 └─ entity linking: client · order · ticket · SKU · email · invoice
                      └─ workflow reconstruction: steps · decisions · exceptions · outcomes
                           └─ summary · SOP · bottleneck · automation opportunity

Key algorithmic capabilities

CapabilityWhat it does
Task segmentationSplits a full day into intelligible work units rather than per-minute fragments.
Entity linkingTies order numbers, ticket IDs, customer names and document references across screens.
Workflow clusteringDetects repeating procedures across multiple people.
Deviation detectionSurfaces paths that deviate from the dominant variant.
Rework detectionFinds duplicate copy-paste, repeated lookups and field churn.
Automation scoringRanks candidates by frequency, regularity, error rate and API feasibility.

Example MVP output

Jane · CSR team — Daily Work Summary. Handled 18 customer order issues today: 7 shipment status checks, 4 address changes, 3 billing inquiries, 2 inventory exceptions, 2 carrier delay follow-ups. Bottleneck: address-change flow toggled between Email, WMS, TMS and the customer portal; avg. handling time 11.3 min, 42% spent on duplicate lookups. Automation candidate: Address Change Assistant — extract order number from email, validate customer permission, call WMS/TMS APIs, prepare an approval step for human review. Generated automatically · evidence linked
VI — Knowledge Mining

Documents say what is true. Screens say what is done.

Enterprise knowledge platforms know what is written in a document, an email, a ticket or a wiki. They generally do not know what an employee actually did with that information. The product’s differentiation is to fuse the two — anchoring every AI answer to both written sources and observed behaviour, always with citation.

Data sources

SourceWhat can be minedValue
Email (Outlook · Gmail)Requests, exceptions, commitments, attachments, contextRecovers the trigger of work
Drive / SharePoint / OneDriveSOPs, contracts, quotes, templates, customer filesFormal knowledge anchors
Screen activityActual paths, fields, human judgementCaptures tacit knowledge
Ticket · CRM · WMS · TMSStructured business entitiesEntity backbone for the graph
Chat (Slack · Teams)Ad-hoc conversations, exceptions, informal loreFills informal knowledge gaps

Knowledge graph schema

Employee  ──handles──>  Task / Case
Task/Case ──belongs_to──> Client / Project
Task/Case ──uses────────> Application / Document / Email
Task/Case ──contains───>  Entity: Order · Shipment · SKU · Invoice · Ticket
Task/Case ──follows────>  Workflow
Workflow  ──has_steps──>  Step
Workflow  ──has_exception──> Exception
Exception ──resolved_by──> Decision / SOP / Human Expert
SOP       ──generated_from──> Screen Evidence + Email Evidence + Document Evidence

Two assistants, one substrate

Personal assistant

“Where did I leave the ABC case yesterday?”

“Generate today’s end-of-day summary.”

“Which email or page showed me this tracking number?”

“List my unfinished items.”

“Draft a training note from cases I handled this week.”

Enterprise assistant

“What is our standard address-change process?”

“Who handles carrier-delay exceptions most often?”

“What are ABC’s most common issues?”

“Why is this workflow taking this long on average?”

“Which processes are the best automation candidates?”

Required · source citation
Every AI answer must cite the screen segment, email, document or system event it was derived from. Answers without source linkage cannot be used for management decisions, audit or process improvement.
VII — Compute Governance

The cloud-desktop layer is also a compute optimisation surface.

The same software that understands work can also explain compute. Because the platform sits inside the VM and observes both human activity and resource consumption, it can recommend VM sizing, suspend idle desktops, route AI inference to the cheapest viable model, and attribute compute cost back to user, project or department. This is structurally impossible for a SaaS recorder that has no relationship with the desktop infrastructure.

Operational questions to answer

  • Which VMs are idle for long periods but still being billed?
  • Which employees actually need GPU-class machines, and which do not?
  • Which AI tasks must use large multimodal models, and which can be solved by small ones?
  • Which screen segments carry no business signal and should not enter expensive inference?
  • How should compute be attributed across users, projects, customers and teams?

Capabilities

CapabilityDescriptionValue
VM idle detectionCombine screen, keyboard / mouse, app and business stateCut idle cloud-desktop spend
Auto suspend / resumePolicy-driven VM lifecycleLower hosting cost
Cost attributionVM / GPU / AI inference by user, project, customerEnables chargeback / showback
AI model routingSmall models pre-filter, big models for keyframes onlyReduce inference unit cost
Edge inferenceOCR · classifier · masking on local / private cloudCost & privacy together
Bundling opportunity
With a hardware partner (Lenovo / Dell / NVIDIA / VDI / GPU server vendors), the platform can be packaged as “AI Desktop Intelligence Appliance” or “Cloud Desktop Cost Governance”. This is the only category in which the product can plausibly sit alongside a six- to seven-figure hardware contract — and a seven-figure contract is already what your existing AI Cloud Desktop quote anticipates.

Architectural principle
Don’t let video become the long-term asset. The long-term assets are structured workflow events, the knowledge graph, SOPs and statistical signals. Video is short-lived evidence behind those structured facts.
IX — Roadmap & Cost

Six phases. Pay only for what unlocks the next price tier.

Build in phases that each unlock a higher price tier. Phase 0 hardens the existing PoC. Phase 1 is the first commercially viable Work Insight MVP. Each subsequent phase opens a new category — workflow, SOP, knowledge, compute, enterprise — and a new buyer.

Minimum saleable version

The first version that can be sold confidently should include VM recording & timeline, app / URL / window stats, OCR plus basic sensitive masking, AI daily work summary, task segmentation, project / client / order / ticket entity recognition, manager dashboard, personal daily report, and basic workflow pattern discovery.

Minimum demo storyline

  1. A logistics CSR receives a customer exception email.
  2. They open WMS / TMS to look up the order and shipment.
  3. The system identifies the client, order number, exception type and steps taken.
  4. A daily summary is auto-generated for the CSR.
  5. An SOP draft is proposed from the captured procedure.
  6. Rework points (duplicate copy-paste and system toggling) are highlighted.
  7. An Address Change Agent or Shipment Status Agent is recommended.
Capital sequencing
Phase 0 + Phase 1 (baseline funding around $200K–$400K) is enough to land the first 50–200 VM pilot. Phases 2–4 should be funded by revenue from those pilots or by anchor enterprise contracts, not by speculative R&D.
X — Pricing & Model

Don’t sell a SaaS seat. Sell a stack.

The product spans software, AI inference, video storage, private deployment and potentially hardware. A single per-seat price will either undercharge or scare buyers. XI — GTM & Wedge

Start where the work is messy and the buyer is the COO.

Don’t sell to every company that has employees. Pick one wedge where workflows are dense, exceptions are common, and SOPs are valuable. The strongest single wedge is logistics back-office workflow intelligence: WMS / TMS / OMS / YMS are operationally complex, exception-driven, and ill-served by both monitoring tools and traditional process mining.

Logistics back-office workflow intelligence. The wedge

Billing reconciliationInvoice · POD · rate · accessorial reconciliationErrors and rework reduction

Stakeholders

  • COO / VP of Operations — cares about cycle time, rework, training and capacity.
  • CIO / IT Director — cares about security, integration, cloud-desktop cost and AI governance.
  • HR / Workforce Manager — cares about employee experience, transparency and compliance.
  • Finance — cares about ROI, cost attribution and provable savings.

Standard one-paragraph pitch

WorkGraph AI helps operations-heavy companies understand how work actually gets done across desktops, enterprise applications, email and shared documents. It does not stop at screenshots or time tracking. It converts real work activity into workflow maps, bottleneck analysis, SOP drafts, personal work memory and automation candidates. For IT teams, it also connects work activity with VM and AI compute utilisation, helping reduce cloud-desktop and inference cost.

XII — Risks & Principles

The hard part is not technology. It is restraint.

Risks

RiskDescriptionMitigation
Bossware framingEmployees and media may read the product as surveillanceLead with personal assistant, aggregate insights, transparent policy
Privacy & complianceRecordings can contain PII, customer data, credentialsMasking, RBAC, retention, audit, consent flows
AI hallucinationWrong summaries can cause management errorsConfidence scores, source citation, human review
Cost runawayNaïve VLM-on-everything destroys marginsKeyframe sampling, small-model pre-filter, only the meaningful frames go to VLM
Enterprise complexitySSO, permissions, isolation, connectors slow deploymentVertical templates first, enterprise hardening later

Product principles

  1. Process improvement is the value. Personal punishment is not.
  2. Employees must receive a tangible benefit — summary, memory, handover, search, less reporting overhead.
  3. Managers default to team and workflow views. Individual replay requires permission and reason.
  4. Every AI conclusion must be traceable to evidence.
  5. Video is short-term evidence. Workflow and knowledge graphs are the long-term asset.
  6. Sensitive masking and retention rules ship on day one, not later.
  7. Focus on one vertical first. Don’t sell as a generic HR SaaS.
  8. The ultimate goal is an AI Skill Factory, not an employee monitor.
Next best actions
(1) Lock the name WorkGraph AI. (2) Choose logistics CSR / back-office as the first vertical. (3) Build three concrete demos — customer exception, billing reconciliation, EDI failure. (4) Upgrade existing recording AI into a task / event / workflow data model. (5) Ship personal daily summary first, so employees feel the value before managers do. (6) Then add the manager dashboard and SOP factory. (7) Finally, integrate email / drive and compute optimisation.
